In 2020, fewer than 200 humanoid robots were deployed anywhere in the world. By end of 2025, that number crossed 16,000 — and 80% were built and deployed in a single country.

This is not a story about incremental progress. This is a deployment curve that has gone near-vertical in less than five years — and a geographic concentration that would, in any other strategic industry, be described as a structural dominance event.

D — Data · O Dimension
Operations · 商业落地

Revenue is not the metric. Deployment density is.

The single most common mistake when evaluating humanoid robotics companies in 2026 is treating revenue as the primary Operations indicator. In this industry, revenue is a lagging indicator of deployment density, which is itself a lagging indicator of data generation — which is the actual competitive moat being built.

Unitree's pricing strategy — the G1 at ~$9,900 — is not a margin sacrifice. It is a deliberate deployment maximization strategy designed to accelerate data accumulation at a pace that no premium-priced competitor can match.

The Operations dimension in 2026 is not a scoreboard of revenue. It is a scoreboard of deployment rate, retention, and data density. By those measures, the leaders are pulling away from the pack faster than any financial metric would suggest.

E — Exclusive Chapter · The Inflection Point
Inside the Flywheel Asymmetry

"The flywheels are not the same size."

All flywheels are not equivalent in structure — they differ not only in speed but in diameter: the breadth of data they capture, the density of signal, and the specificity of intelligence improvements enabled.

Flywheel A · China Model
Volume / Task-Specific
Large number of robots in concentrated industrial tasks. Vast data quantity, high task density. Intelligence improvements are real and compounding — but narrow. Optimized for known environments.

Unitree task distribution: ~85% in 5 core industrial categories

Flywheel B · U.S. Model
Breadth / Generalization
Fewer robots across deliberately diverse environments — homes, offices, clinical, varied manufacturing. Lower data volume. Higher generalization signal per unit. Learning to navigate genuine environmental diversity.

1X Technologies: 40+ distinct environment/task categories

If the next five years are primarily about optimizing known industrial tasks at scale, China's flywheel wins. If they are about developing robots that operate across the full diversity of human environments, the generalization flywheel becomes the decisive asset.

This is the question that 2026 is in the process of answering.

E — Analyst Verdict
Where the analysis lands

The race is not decided. The scoreboard is real.

CHINA

China enters 2026 with structural advantages that are real, compounding, and — in the near term — widening. The deployment density advantage is measurable and documented. The integrated industrial ecosystem — policy design, supply chain depth, deployment environment access, standards architecture — has been assembled with deliberate intent. Chinese humanoid companies are generating proprietary operational data at rates that no Western competitor has matched.

UNITED STATES

The United States enters 2026 with technical depth that is genuine, a capital ecosystem globally unmatched, and enterprise customer sophistication that creates structured commercial pathways. These are durable advantages. They are also insufficient — on their own — to close a deployment gap now measured not in months but in operational data years. The private-capital model is not optimized to produce the deployment density required to win the data competition.

THE OUTCOME

The race is not decided. But the window for strategic clarity is shorter than most participants are behaving as if it is. The companies that will define this industry in 2030 are, with very few exceptions, already in this index. The question is which of their current advantages will prove most durable — and which will prove most vulnerable to the competitive dynamics that 2026 has now set in motion.
