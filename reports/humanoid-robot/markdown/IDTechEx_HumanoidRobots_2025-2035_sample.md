Pain Points and trends of humanoid robots

Lack of training data: humanoid robots require massive real working scenario data for training, such as joint movement trajectories and sensor
data for force control and tactile feedback, which is different from the data used for LLMs like ChatGPT. Using synesthetic data, such as Nvidia’s
Isaac Sim seems to be one of the solutions to address this pain point where one set of movements from human operators can be copied and
duplicated to hundreds of datasets.

From specialized to universal robots: many humanoid robots are designed to be used for general purposes. However, the current status of
technology as of early 2025 is still far from general-purpose humanoid robots. IDTechEx believes that over the short-term future (1-3 years),
humanoid robots will likely find usage in a specialized context. For a longer term (5-8 years), humanoids might be used more widely for general
tasks.

The absence of standardized components: Like the automotive industry, the humanoid robotics sector relies on a long and complex supply chain.
However, unlike automotive components, the humanoid robotics market remains fragmented, with a strong emphasis on customized products.
Additionally, while the automotive industry benefits from established Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers, the humanoid robotics sector has very few, if any,
module OEMs that integrate various components for downstream assembly. Nonetheless, this gap presents an opportunity for humanoid robot
OEMs to pursue vertical integration, enabling them to incorporate components in-house and potentially lower costs.

Potential for in-house component development: Although core components can be sourced from mature suppliers, in-house production is crucial
for rapid iteration and the efficient development of humanoid robots. This is especially important in the early stages when quick development cycles
are required to refine and optimize designs.

Tactile sensing might get increasingly essential: vision accounts for only 10-20% of how a humanoid understands its surroundings. As a result,
humanoid robots require dexterous hands and tactile sensors to learn worker skills. Therefore, tactile feedback has been a pain point in the
humanoid robotics industry for reasons including performance limitations of tactile sensing hardware, lack of integration and standardized tactile
data in the industry, as well as a lack of highly adaptive algorithms to parse various tactile dimensions in changing environments.

Benefits and challenges of humanoid robots in the logistics industry

Benefits

Challenges

1. Addressing labor shortages: Similar to one of the main

reasons that warehouse owners want to adopt automation, lack
of skilled labor can potentially be mitigated by using humanoid
robots.

1. Limited deployment and ROI concern: Current deployment is limited; the commercial
deployment is very low (<5% of deployment rate) as of 2025. This also means that the
industry does not have enough evidence to prove the efficiency improvement of introducing
humanoid robots. Warehouse owners lack evidence payback time.

2. Enhancing efficiency: These robots perform tasks with

precision and consistency, minimizing errors and improving
operational speed.

3. Cost reduction: By automating repetitive tasks, humanoid robots
can lower labor expenses and reduce operational disruptions.

4. Flexibility: Unlike specialized robots, humanoids can be

repurposed for different tasks throughout the day, adapting to
changing business needs.

5. Space optimization: Advanced sensors and AI allow humanoid

robots to efficiently navigate and utilize warehouse space,
potentially improving storage density. The warehouse is designed
for human operators so humanoid robots would interact with the
environment more naturally.

6. Continuous operation: Humanoid robots can work continuously
without breaks, significantly increasing operational hours and
productivity.

2. Technology limitation: The technology is still maturing, and real-world traction remains
modest. For instance, the typical operating time per humanoid robot is only 2 to 3 hours,
and it takes around 2 to 3 hours to be fully recharged, so this leads to a downtime of 50%.
IDTechEx believes that fast-charging will likely be the solution to enable shorter downtime,
but existing battery technology used in humanoid robots as of 2025 degrades with frequent
fast charging. While there are batteries with a lifespan much less impacted by fast charging,
they come with higher costs, posing an additional challenge for adoption.

3. Necessity of humanoids in warehouses: Some experts question whether humanoid form

factors are optimal for all warehouse tasks.

4. High CAPEX: The capital costs of humanoid robots are very high, with Tesla Optimus and
Agility Robotics Digit costing around US$100,000 and UBTech’s Walker S costing over
US$20,000 as of early 2025. Despite the cost decreases with rising volume, IDTechEx
believes that the capital cost remains relatively high for warehouse owners.

5. Long testing duration: Amazon, one of the earliest adopters of humanoid robots in

warehouses, reports that it takes around 18 months for pilot testing of humanoid robots in
warehouses before they get largely adopted, like the adoption curve of AGVs at their early
stage.

Design and manufacturing challenges – motors and thermal management
(1/2)

Within humanoid robots, there are a few types of commonly used motors, including coreless motors, direct drive motors, framed and frameless
motors, brushed and brushless DC motors. Categorized by driving mechanisms, motors can be electrical, pneumatic, and hydraulic.

Heat generated around joints

Continuous operation of 40+ joints generates 500–800W heat (e.g., 10-20 W per BLDC motor at 50 N·m), requiring liquid
cooling systems that add 10–15% mass. Unitree G1’s passive air-cooling limits runtime to 45 minutes under full load.

Coreless motors

Coreless motors achieve high torque density by eliminating the iron core, reducing weight and inertia. Their small footprint
usually makes them suitable for areas requiring compactness, such as, hands and fingers. However, this lack of iron core
limits torque output compared to iron-core motors of similar size. Therefore, it’s a balance between the total torque output
and size/weight of the motor. Additionally, heat dissipation is also a critical challenge for coreless motors. The absence of
iron core reduces heat dissipation capacity, increasing the risks of overheating during high-power operation. Therefore,
advanced cooling methods, such as, forced air cooling and liquid cooling might be needed, leading to an increased system
complexity. The lack of solid iron core also increases the mechanical fragility as the thin coils themselves are prone to
deformation under stress.

Design, manufacturing, and commercial challenges – tactile sensors

For tactile sensing, there are plenty of technologies available, including capacitive, magnetic, and optical. The challenges are as follows:

High cost

Regardless of the technologies (capacitive, magnetic, or optical), the cost per tactile sensor is relatively high (between US$300 and US$500, depending on the volume,
customer relationship, and other factors). The high cost presents a significant barrier to large volume adoption of tactile sensors for humanoids hands. When it comes
to evaluating robotic electronic skin, the total skin area of an adult is 1.8 m2, and considering the unit price of the Flexi Force series A101 flexible piezoresistive tactile
sensor from Tekscan, IDTechEx estimated that electronic skin costs around US$42.9/cm2, which is around 40% lower than tactile sensors. It is also worth noting that
this cost is just a rough estimation, and the costs vary largely depending on the suppliers.

Immature manufacturing and potential improvements on the yield rate

The manufacturing process is not mature yet as of 2025, therefore, the yield is not high enough. However, IDTechEx believes that if the manufacturing process
becomes stable and reliable and the productivity can be increased significantly, the costs per tactile sensor will decrease significantly.

Materials – bounce rate

One of the critical challenges of tactile sensors is the flexible dielectric materials/contact layer (e.g., PDMS film). Flexible materials need to have good flexibility, rebound
consistency (critical when gripping multiple times repeatedly, directly affecting the performance and reliability of the tactile sensors), and aging resistance (to maintain
good condition and performance in extremely cold, high and low temperature environments). These are the bottlenecks of the current tactile sensors.

Flexible tactile sensors

Flexible tactile sensors are increasingly favored in humanoid robotics for their superior conformability to complex, curved surfaces, allowing seamless integration and
more natural interactions with the environment compared to rigid sensors. These sensors have high sensitivity, spatial resolution, and multi-modal capabilities, enabling
robots to detect stimuli like pressure, temperature, and humidity, which significantly enhances dexterous manipulation and object recognition. However, they face
challenges such as faster degradation in high-contact areas and difficulties in detecting shear (tangential) forces, which are essential for securely handling flexible
objects without causing damage or slippage. While capacitive sensing excels at measuring normal forces, its limitations in shear force detection highlight the potential
need for hybrid or alternative technologies to address these drawbacks and fully unlock the potential of flexible tactile sensors in advanced robotic applications.

How to work around safety and regulatory requirement – cooperative space
for industrial settings

According to Agility Robotics, the pilot trails with Stellantis still requires the robots to work in a confined space separated from human operators
to meet the safety requirements and avoid physical contacts with humans. According to Agility, as of early 2025, there is no humanoid robot on
the market that meets the requirement to work outside an isolated space, separated from people.

According to Agility, the ultimate goal is to achieve cooperative safety. This means humanoid robots and humans work in the same space but
they are not working on the same item at the same time. In this case, humanoid robots can be freed from the cage so that they can utilize their
autonomous mobility potential.

It is worth noting that cooperative working and
collaborative working is slightly different where
collaborative working means that robots and humans
can work on the same item, at the same time and
within the same area whereas cooperative safety
usually refers to a transitionary stage between
traditional robots that are caged and fully
collaborative robots.

However, Agility Robotics reported that within 24
months (early 2027), they will have a fully
collaborative safety rated robot that is able to work in
a warehouse or manufacturing floor without cages in
the USA and EU.

Humanoid’s batteries – parameters comparison

Humanoid robotics is a fundamentally complicated system.
Its dynamic motion requires high torque from actuators,
leading to a rapid battery drain. For instance, Unitree’s G1
operate for around 2 hours and Honda’s ASIMO also
achieves ~1-2 hours of operation, which necessitates
breakthroughs in energy-dense power sources.

Most current humanoid robots available in the market have
a battery capacity between 0.5kWh and 1.25kWh with a
few high-performance ones such as Tesla Optimus, Figure
AI’s Figure 02, Apptronik’s Appollo, and Xiaomi’s
CyberOne. Despite that most humanoid robots on this
chart are designed to be general-purposed, it can be
observed that the ones with high performance are mostly
designed for the automotive factories as their first step to
commercializing.

It is worth noting that these numbers are based off the
figures provided by the companies, and in reality, the
operational time could vary depending on the workload.
For instance, a humanoid robot might only be able to
operate for 50 minutes under full power whereas it can run
for up to 3 hours under the standby (low-power) mode.
